# Rohit Kapoor — Angel Investor Review

---

## Positioning assessment

The first question I ask when I see a personal brand site: *is this person building something or selling their time?*

After reading this site twice, I'm not sure. The copy says "General Manager at a political consulting firm" — so he's an employee? Or is this his firm? "360-degree advisory services to a national party" — is the firm his? Or is he the GM of someone else's firm? This ambiguity is a positioning problem. A site like this should make the answer obvious in the first paragraph. It doesn't.

If this is his firm: lead with that. "I founded and run [firm name]" is a very different signal than "I am the General Manager of."

If he's an employee building a personal brand alongside his job: that's a different kind of site and needs different framing.

Right now it reads like neither and both, which is the worst outcome.

---

## Credibility signals — present and absent

**Present:**
- MBA from IIFT Delhi — credible, recognised institution
- Design quality — signals that this person takes presentation seriously
- "Zero-to-one firm-building" — hints at founder-level involvement
- The dark CTA band copy is confident and specific to the political context

**Absent:**
- No election outcomes — which cycles, which states, what role
- No scale indicators — team size, number of clients, years in operation
- No named references — not even an industry association or publication mention
- No firm name — anonymising the firm means I can't verify anything
- No LinkedIn — the first thing I do after reading a site like this

The stats provided — "360°", "3", "0→1" — are not stats. They are design elements masquerading as data. "3 Core Capabilities" counts items on the same page. "0→1" is a frame that means different things to different people. "360°" is a marketing claim without a referent. Investors pattern-match on real numbers: elections worked, research briefs delivered, years operating, team headcount. None of these are present.

---

## The testimonials problem

Two anonymous quotes. No names. No organisations. In political consulting, where every engagement is theoretically confidential, *some* level of attribution is still possible and expected. "Former [State] Minister" or "Senior official, national party" or even just a first name and role tells me there's a real person behind the quote. "Senior Leadership — Political Consulting" tells me there might not be.

If these quotes are real, fix the attribution. If they're illustrative, remove them entirely. Fabricated-looking testimonials on an otherwise high-quality site create a jarring credibility gap.

---

## The positioning question no one's answering

This site claims to offer research, program management, and "executive assistancy" (a term I had to look up — it's not standard in Indian corporate or political circles; the equivalent is chief of staff or EA). But it never answers the implicit question: *why Tarang specifically, and not one of the twenty other well-credentialled operators in the same space?*

"Rare combination" — how many times have I read that on a consultant's site? The MBA plus corporate experience plus startup experience story is not rare. Every third founder I meet has this profile. What IS rare here, specifically? What has Tarang done that nobody else has done? That answer is in the CLAUDE.md context (zero-to-one in a live national election cycle) but it doesn't translate convincingly into the site copy.

---

## Contact infrastructure

No email address. No phone. No LinkedIn. Form only. This is incongruent with the "direct line, no intake forms" positioning. If the brand message is directness and speed, the contact method should match. One email address, prominently placed, would do more for positioning than any copy on the page.

---

## Would I bet on this person based on this site?

If someone I respected sent me here: the site would confirm they have polish and can communicate. It wouldn't tell me much more.

Cold: no. Nothing is verifiable. The positioning is ambiguous. The social proof is weak. The stats are decorative. The ambiguity around whether this is his firm or his employer's firm is a genuine flag.

The potential is visible. The proof is absent.

---

## One fix that would change everything

Clarify in one sentence: *who Tarang is in relation to this work* (founder, principal, GM-for-hire), *at what scale* (one national party, multiple parties, how many election cycles), and *one verifiable outcome*. That single paragraph, if honest and specific, would do more for credibility than the entire visual design.
