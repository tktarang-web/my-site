## My honest reaction, section by section

**Hero**
"Your campaign runs. Your decisions land." — I read this and I think: everyone says this. My current consultant says this. The guy before him said this. What I want to know in 5 seconds is: has this person done this before, for someone like me, and did it actually work? This headline doesn't answer that. It's a promise, not a proof.

I scrolled immediately because there was nothing else to do on this page.

**About**
"Most consultants tell you what to do. I make sure it gets done." — okay, this is different. I'm reading.

"MBA from IIFT Delhi. High-level corporate experience. Zero-to-one firm-building in live political environments." — the MBA I understand. The corporate experience I understand. "Zero-to-one firm-building in live political environments" — what does this mean? He built a firm? He helped build one? Which party? Which elections? I'm now less sure, not more.

"General Manager of a political consulting firm providing 360-degree advisory services to a national party." — which party? I work in this space. If I can't identify the firm or the party, I have no way to verify. This could be anyone.

I need ONE piece of verifiable information. One election cycle. One named outcome. Even "worked on the [state] assembly elections in [year]" would tell me something real.

**Services**
The three services are described well. I understand what "Original Research" means in this context. "Program Management" is clear. "Executive Assistancy" — I had to read this twice. I think he means what I'd call a senior EA or chief of staff. I'd want to confirm before I call.

The "outcome" lines at the bottom of each card are good. "Leadership walks into rooms informed." That's exactly what I need.

**Testimonials**
"Senior Leadership — Political Consulting."
"Partner — Election Strategy, National Party."

I don't know these people. I have no way to know these people. In a sector where every referral comes through someone you've met at least twice, anonymous quotes mean nothing. I immediately thought: are these real? That thought should never happen on a site that wants my trust.

**Contact section**
"Direct line. No intake forms." — but there is a form. I noticed this. It made me feel slightly like the copy was written to sound good rather than to be accurate.

Five fields is a lot for a first message. I would typically call or have someone call on my behalf. There is no phone number here. There is no email address. There is only this form. That is a barrier for me.

"By referral and direct inquiry." — this is the right positioning. It tells me this is not a mass-market service. But I found this by accident in the footer column. It should be up front.

## Questions I still have after reading the whole site

1. Who specifically has Tarang worked with? (Not asking for names — just scale, party type, election cycle)
2. Is he currently available or fully committed elsewhere?
3. What does engagement look like? Retainer? Project-based? What's the rough cost signal?
4. Who else on his team would I be working with? (He mentions managing direct reports — am I getting Tarang or his team?)
5. Why should I trust someone I can't verify?

---

## Would I contact him?

If someone I trusted had referred me to this site: yes, I'd fill the form.

If I found this cold: probably not. The site is beautifully made but it doesn't give me enough to feel confident making a first move. I'd keep it in mind and wait until I ran into someone who knew him.

---

## Bottom line

The site looks like someone serious. But looking serious and being verifiable are different things. One concrete proof point — one named context, one real attribution, one direct contact method — would close the gap between "this seems credible" and "I'm going to reach out today."
